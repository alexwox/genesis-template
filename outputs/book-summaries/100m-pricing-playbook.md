## Tactical Ideas
### 28-day billing quietly raises annual revenue
- **ID:** `100m-pricing-playbook-004`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps recurring-revenue businesses collect more over a year without a blunt headline price increase.
- **How It Works:** Switching from monthly billing to every 28 days creates an extra billing cycle over the year. The play is operationally simple but economically meaningful for the right business.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A gym or membership community can move from monthly autopay to every-28-days billing to increase annual cash collected per member.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Pricing Play #1: Monthly To 28 Day Billing Cycles
- **Related Items:** None

### Pass through costs instead of silently eating them
- **ID:** `100m-pricing-playbook-005`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps businesses protect margin from payment processing fees, taxes, and similar leaks.
- **How It Works:** Several pricing plays focus on shifting unavoidable cost burdens back into the pricing model rather than allowing small leaks to accumulate unnoticed. The principle is that hidden margin loss compounds fast.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A merchant can add processing-fee recovery, a second payment method, or explicit tax handling rather than letting those costs quietly compress gross profit.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Pricing Play #2 Processing Fees & Second Form of Payment; Pricing Play #3 Sales Tax
- **Related Items:** None

### Make annual increases a built-in habit
- **ID:** `100m-pricing-playbook-006`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps businesses avoid the pain of huge step changes by normalizing regular price maintenance.
- **How It Works:** The annual increase play is about making repricing routine instead of waiting until the business is in trouble. Smaller, regular changes are easier to absorb than long stretches of avoidance followed by panic.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A software company can set a yearly review and notify customers of a modest annual adjustment tied to product improvements and inflation rather than freezing prices for years.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Pricing Play #4 Annual Price Increases
- **Related Items:** None

### Anchor value upward before the main offer
- **ID:** `100m-pricing-playbook-008`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps the core offer feel more reasonable and valuable by showing a much more expensive reference point first.
- **How It Works:** The ultra-high-ticket anchor and guarantee or warranty upsell plays raise perceived value and improve monetization without discounting the core. The idea is to expand the pricing context around the buyer.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - An agency could show a high-end done-for-you package first, making the standard package look like the sensible middle choice, then add a paid guarantee extension for buyers who want extra assurance.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Pricing Play #9 Ultra High Ticket Anchor; #10 Guarantee and Warranty Upsells
- **Related Items:** None

## Strategic Ideas
### Profit is unnatural, so force it
- **ID:** `100m-pricing-playbook-001`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps owners stop assuming profit appears automatically if the business simply gets bigger.
- **How It Works:** The playbook opens with the idea that profit must be engineered. Pricing decisions are therefore active design choices, not passive reflections of what everyone else charges.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A founder can treat pricing as a scheduled operating system review rather than a number that stays untouched for years.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Guiding Principles; Pricing To Make The Most Money
- **Related Items:** None

### Price based on economics, not broke competitors
- **ID:** `100m-pricing-playbook-003`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps businesses avoid anchoring to a market where everyone is undercharging.
- **How It Works:** Hormozi's pricing rules are built around business economics and customer value rather than competitor averages. Following underpriced peers usually keeps a business stuck in the same weak economics they have.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A local service business can study its fulfillment cost, gross profit, and customer value instead of simply charging the middle of the market because that feels safe.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Three Models of Pricing; My Rules For Pricing
- **Related Items:** None

## Frameworks
### Not all ways of doubling the business are equal
- **ID:** `100m-pricing-playbook-002`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps businesses compare customer growth, repeat purchase, and price changes based on profit impact instead of just revenue impact.
- **How It Works:** The genie thought experiment shows that doubling customers, doubling purchase frequency, and doubling price all affect profit differently. The book pushes owners to look at margin and cash consequences, not topline symmetry.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A subscription business might discover that a price increase or renewal improvement creates more profit than doubling acquisition while fulfillment costs rise.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Pricing To Make The Most Money
- **Related Items:** None

### Use payment structure as a pricing tool
- **ID:** `100m-pricing-playbook-007`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps owners increase cash and retention by changing how buyers pay, not just what they pay.
- **How It Works:** Annual billing, renewal fees, automatic continuity, and payment framing all affect customer behavior and cash flow. The book treats these structures as profit levers, not just checkout options.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A membership business can offer annual prepay, attach a renewal fee, or roll buyers into continuity to improve both cash position and expected retention.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Pricing Play #5 Annual Billing; #7 Annual Renewal Fee; #8 Automatic Continuity
- **Related Items:** None
