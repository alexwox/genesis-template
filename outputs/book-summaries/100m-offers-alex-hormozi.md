## Tactical Ideas
### Map all problems and all solutions before packaging
- **ID:** `100m-offers-alex-hormozi-005`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps offer builders uncover what the buyer really needs before deciding what to include.
- **How It Works:** Hormozi recommends listing every obstacle, every step, and every before-and-after issue the buyer encounters. Only then should the offer be assembled, because better packaging starts from a fuller picture of the buyer's path.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A course creator could map not only what students need to learn, but also motivation drops, setup hurdles, implementation fears, and post-win next problems before finalizing the offer.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Creating Your Grand Slam Offer Part I: Problems & Solutions
- **Related Items:** None

## Strategic Ideas
### Start with a starving crowd
- **ID:** `100m-offers-alex-hormozi-001`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps founders avoid building offers for markets that are too indifferent, too poor, or too hard to move.
- **How It Works:** The book repeatedly emphasizes that the market matters as much as the offer. It is easier to sell to people with painful, expensive, urgent problems than to persuade an apathetic audience to care.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A business choosing between hobby knitters and owners of failing home-service companies should usually prefer the latter if the goal is to sell premium help quickly.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Pricing; Finding The Right Market -- A Starving Crowd
- **Related Items:** None

### Charge what it is worth, not what competitors charge
- **ID:** `100m-offers-alex-hormozi-004`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps businesses escape commodity pricing and the margin trap created by competitor-led pricing.
- **How It Works:** The pricing chapters argue that competitors are a bad reference point because many of them are undercharging and underperforming. The better question is what the result is worth to the customer and how strongly the offer delivers it.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A recruiting agency that helps a company avoid a six-figure bad hire should not price itself based on hourly labor rates alone.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Pricing: The Commodity Problem; Pricing: Charge What It’s Worth
- **Related Items:** None

## Frameworks
### Grand Slam Offer
- **ID:** `100m-offers-alex-hormozi-002`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps businesses design offers so compelling that the buyer feels stupid saying no.
- **How It Works:** Hormozi's Grand Slam Offer is the combination of pricing, value creation, risk reduction, and offer presentation that makes the purchase unusually attractive relative to alternatives. It is not one tactic; it is the whole package working together.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A business can combine a sharp promise, clear delivery path, proof, guarantee, and high-value bonuses into one offer that makes a standard competitor look weak.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Grand Slam Offers; Start Here
- **Related Items:** None

### Value Equation
- **ID:** `100m-offers-alex-hormozi-003`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps teams increase perceived value in a structured way instead of guessing what buyers will pay more for.
- **How It Works:** Value equals dream outcome times perceived likelihood of achievement divided by time delay and effort or sacrifice. Better offers either raise the desirability and certainty of the result or reduce the friction and waiting required to get it.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A bookkeeping service could increase value by promising faster cash-flow visibility, adding proof from similar businesses, shortening onboarding time, and minimizing owner effort with done-for-you data collection.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Value Offer: The Value Equation
- **Related Items:** None

### Use the delivery cube to generate offer components
- **ID:** `100m-offers-alex-hormozi-006`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps businesses find higher-value delivery combinations instead of defaulting to one standard format.
- **How It Works:** The delivery cube expands thinking across group ratio, buyer effort, support level, medium, and speed. It is a structured way to invent delivery variations that can raise perceived value or improve margins.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A consultant can compare DIY templates, done-with-you workshops, and done-for-you implementation with different support channels to find the best balance of attractiveness and scalability.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Delivery thinking; Delivery Cube concepts
- **Related Items:** None

### Trim and stack
- **ID:** `100m-offers-alex-hormozi-007`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps businesses keep the highest-value components and remove low-value complexity from the offer.
- **How It Works:** After generating many possible components, the next move is to trim what is slow, weak, or costly and stack the strongest pieces into one compelling package. The offer should feel richer to the buyer while staying deliverable for the business.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A service business might remove low-impact weekly calls but stack a kickoff intensive, a KPI dashboard, and a premium troubleshooting channel because those create more perceived value.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Creating Your Grand Slam Offer Part II: Trim & Stack
- **Related Items:** None

### Enhancers: scarcity, urgency, bonuses, guarantees, naming
- **ID:** `100m-offers-alex-hormozi-008`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps strong offers convert faster by increasing perceived value and reducing hesitation at the decision moment.
- **How It Works:** Once the core offer is solid, the conversion enhancers make it more compelling. Scarcity limits access, urgency compresses action, bonuses solve objections, guarantees reduce risk, and naming improves memorability and perceived specificity.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A high-ticket program could cap intake, set a cohort deadline, add implementation bonuses, include a performance guarantee, and give the package a concrete transformation-focused name.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Enhancing The Offer; Scarcity; Urgency; Bonuses; Guarantees; Naming
- **Related Items:** None
