## Strategic Ideas
### Stage 0: Improvise
- **ID:** `100m-scaling-roadmap-combined-002`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps pre-revenue operators focus on getting people to try the thing instead of pretending they already run a company.
- **How It Works:** Stage 0 is about free usage, feedback, simple tools, and learning what is worth charging for. The goal is evidence of interest, not polished infrastructure.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A new consultant can offer early free engagements, gather feedback, and learn what clients actually value before formal pricing and systems.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Stage 0 Improvise
- **Related Items:** None

### Stage 1: Monetize
- **ID:** `100m-scaling-roadmap-combined-003`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps early operators shift from free validation to first paid demand.
- **How It Works:** The main job is to make the first sale consistently, build a version-one product that is good enough to pay for, and learn to ask for money. Proof from free users becomes the first sales asset.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A creator with a free community can launch a simple paid cohort and use feedback plus early wins to validate willingness to pay.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Stage 1 Monetize
- **Related Items:** None

### Stage 2: Advertise
- **ID:** `100m-scaling-roadmap-combined-004`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps businesses move from founder hustle to repeatable demand creation.
- **How It Works:** Once people will pay, the next step is learning how to generate demand systematically. The stage exists to make customer flow less dependent on sporadic referrals or personal outreach alone.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A services business that can already sell from warm leads can begin testing ads or content systems that produce opportunities on demand.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Stage 2 Advertise
- **Related Items:** None

### Stage 3: Stabilize
- **ID:** `100m-scaling-roadmap-combined-005`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps businesses stop breaking delivery and operations every time demand increases.
- **How It Works:** At this point the company must make the existing motion more reliable: delivery quality, customer experience, basic systems, and economic consistency matter more than raw experimentation.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A fast-growing agency can pause before the next growth push to standardize onboarding, reporting, and fulfillment quality.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Stage 3 Stabilize
- **Related Items:** None

### Stage 4: Prioritize
- **ID:** `100m-scaling-roadmap-combined-006`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps leaders avoid being busy across too many initiatives when the company finally has enough activity to create real trade-offs.
- **How It Works:** The roadmap implies that scaling starts to fail when everything looks important at once. Prioritization becomes the move that protects the highest-leverage work from organizational noise.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A founder with several channels, offers, and hires in flight can cut half the initiatives and concentrate on the one constraint most likely to unlock the next stage.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Stage 4 Prioritize
- **Related Items:** None

### Stage 5: Productize
- **ID:** `100m-scaling-roadmap-combined-007`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps owner-dependent delivery become a standardized, repeatable product.
- **How It Works:** As businesses scale, custom work and founder improvisation become expensive. Productizing creates consistency, easier training, and cleaner economics.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A consulting business can turn one-off solutions into clearer packages, playbooks, and standardized delivery paths.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Stage 5 Productize
- **Related Items:** None

### Stage 6: Optimize
- **ID:** `100m-scaling-roadmap-combined-008`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps the company improve the efficiency of proven systems before chasing new complexity.
- **How It Works:** Optimization is about tightening the machine: conversion rates, margins, staffing, retention, and cycle times. Small percentage improvements matter more once the base is larger.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - An ecommerce company can improve checkout conversion, return rates, and repeat purchase before launching another channel.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Stage 6 Optimize
- **Related Items:** None

### Stage 7 and 8: Categorize then Specialize
- **ID:** `100m-scaling-roadmap-combined-009`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps larger companies organize talent and functions clearly enough to support specialization without chaos.
- **How It Works:** These later stages imply a shift from generalist hustle to role clarity, clearer ownership, and more specialized teams. The org has to know who owns what before deeper specialization adds value.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A growing portfolio brand can split acquisition, retention, ops, and finance ownership cleanly before hiring more narrowly specialized leaders inside those functions.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Stage 7 Categorize; Stage 8 Specialize
- **Related Items:** None

### Stage 9: Capitalize
- **ID:** `100m-scaling-roadmap-combined-010`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps mature businesses convert operational strength into enterprise value and compounding opportunity.
- **How It Works:** The final stage is about extracting the full strategic and financial upside of a well-built business. The company is no longer just surviving stage by stage; it is positioned to compound, invest, and capitalize on scale.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A mature company with stable systems, management depth, and strong unit economics can pursue rollups, new capital structures, or more aggressive expansion from a position of strength.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Stage 9 Capitalize
- **Related Items:** None

## Frameworks
### Scaling is a stage progression, not one generic problem
- **ID:** `100m-scaling-roadmap-combined-001`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps operators solve the right bottleneck for their current size instead of borrowing advice from a different stage.
- **How It Works:** The roadmap organizes growth into explicit stages, each with its own role, headcount, constraint, and graduation standard. Advice that works one stage later can be wasteful or harmful now.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A solo founder with no sales should not prioritize executive scorecards before proving that anyone will pay for the offer.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Start Here; Scaling Roadmap overview
- **Related Items:** None
