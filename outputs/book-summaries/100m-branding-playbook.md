## Tactical Ideas
### Associate your brand with what the avatar already values
- **ID:** `100m-branding-playbook-005`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps businesses build brand meaning faster by borrowing relevance from existing interests instead of waiting for slow organic recognition.
- **How It Works:** After identifying the avatar, the next move is to map what they already watch, trust, admire, buy, and talk about. Then the brand deliberately appears next to those ideas through content, people, products, and environments.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A B2B software company selling to operators could publish teardown content on the same podcasts, metrics, and operating systems its audience already follows so the brand becomes mentally grouped with practical operators rather than generic SaaS noise.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Step 2 Figure Out What They Like; Step 3 Associate Your Brand With Relevant Things They Like
- **Related Items:** None

## Strategic Ideas
### Branding grows customers, value, and durability
- **ID:** `100m-branding-playbook-002`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps owners justify brand investment by connecting it to the three economic outcomes that matter most.
- **How It Works:** The book frames branding as one of the few levers that can lower CAC, raise LTV, and make demand persist over time. The point is not 'looking premium' but making acquisition cheaper, pricing stronger, and loyalty deeper.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A coaching company could use consistent customer-result stories and category-specific authority content to improve conversion on ads, charge more than commodity competitors, and launch new products into an already warm audience.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Branding; Why it makes money; Big Brand = Big Profits
- **Related Items:** None

### Branding should change behavior, not just opinion
- **ID:** `100m-branding-playbook-003`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps teams distinguish branding that feels interesting from branding that actually changes customer action.
- **How It Works:** The playbook uses a learning-and-behavior-change lens: if the same situation produces new behavior, branding worked. This forces the brand to be judged by outcomes like clickthroughs, purchases, referrals, and repeat intent rather than vibes.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A founder can test whether a new trust-heavy messaging angle works by watching whether inbound prospects mention credibility, authority, or safety more often in sales calls after the campaign launches.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Learning and Behavior Change
- **Related Items:** None

## Frameworks
### Branding as deliberate association
- **ID:** `100m-branding-playbook-001`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps businesses stop treating branding like vague aesthetics and start using it to shape buyer perception intentionally.
- **How It Works:** Hormozi defines branding as pairing your business with things your ideal customer already likes. Good branding creates favorable associations on purpose; bad branding pairs you with things your market dislikes or ignores.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A legal-services company serving assisted-living operators could consistently pair itself with trusted retirement-planning content, practical compliance guides, and respected elder-care voices instead of generic corporate messaging.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Branding; Branding As I See It; What Branding Is NOT
- **Related Items:** None

### Choose the right audience before building the brand
- **ID:** `100m-branding-playbook-004`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps businesses avoid branding to a market that cannot buy, is hard to reach, or is shrinking.
- **How It Works:** Hormozi recommends picking an avatar that is growing, in pain, can afford the offer, and is easy to find. Better branding on a bad market still underperforms weak branding on a structurally attractive market.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - An estate-planning firm could prioritize assisted-living operators over general consumers because the operators are easier to target, feel the pain regularly, and can pay for a specialized service.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: How to Start, Grow, then Pivot Your Own Brand; Step 1 Figure Out Who Your Ideal Customer Is
- **Related Items:** None
