# $100M Hooks Playbook

## Executive Snapshot
This playbook treats hooks as the sale of attention. The key ideas are that a hook has two jobs, `call out` and `value condition`, that hooks should be mined from winning data rather than dreamed up in isolation, and that a portfolio approach (`70/20/10`) keeps output stable while still creating room for breakout formats.

## Tactical Ideas
### Spend Most Advertising Time On Hooks
Hormozi treats hook work as the highest-leverage part of creative. If the audience does not make it through the first seconds, nothing downstream matters.

### Build A Hook Library From Winners
Create a spreadsheet by platform, store top-performing hooks, and reuse them across formats. Winning tweets can become short videos, ad hooks can become email subject lines, and top content can become paid creative.

### Let Data Teach The Next Batch
If there is no prior dataset, publish anyway and let the top 10 percent of outcomes teach what to do more of. The book's bias is toward iteration from evidence rather than waiting for expert certainty.

## Strategic Ideas
### Hooks Are The First Impression That Decides Everything Else
A hook is not decoration. It is the first sale, the part that gets the prospect to keep consuming the message.

### Good Hooks Are Specific Enough To Call Out The Right People But Broad Enough To Scale
The best hooks create recognition without over-narrowing the audience. This matters for ads, content, and outbound, because better hooks improve both volume and quality of attention.

### Treat Hook Development As Portfolio Management
Most effort should go to what already works, some effort to adjacent patterns, and a small amount to true experiments. That mix protects current performance while still creating future winners.

## Frameworks
### Callout Plus Value Condition
A hook combines a callout that makes the right person think `this is for me` and a value condition that promises some benefit if they keep paying attention. This is the book's base model for constructing hooks across media.

### Verbal And Nonverbal Hook Types
Verbal hooks include labels, questions, conditionals, commands, statements, lists, narratives, and provocative exclamations. Nonverbal hooks use visuals and sounds to trigger the same attention response.

### 70/20/10 Hook Allocation
Put 70 percent of effort into core proven hooks, 20 percent into adjacent ideas that resemble winners, and 10 percent into genuinely new experiments. When a 10 percent experiment wins, move it into the 70 percent bucket.
