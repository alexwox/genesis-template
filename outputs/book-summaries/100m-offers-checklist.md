## Tactical Ideas
### Choose a market before choosing an offer
- **ID:** `100m-offers-checklist-001`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps businesses avoid polishing an offer for a market with weak demand or poor economics.
- **How It Works:** The checklist starts with market and niche selection: pain, purchasing power, easy targeting, and growth. This makes market choice the first offer-design decision.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A founder can rule out a fun niche with low purchasing power and instead choose a smaller but more monetizable segment that is actively looking for help.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Picking The Right Market/Niche
- **Related Items:** None

### Write every problem before you design the package
- **ID:** `100m-offers-checklist-003`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps the offer solve the real job instead of only the founder's favorite part of the problem.
- **How It Works:** The checklist asks the builder to map dream outcome, every obstacle, every step, and what happens before and after the goal. This pushes deeper buyer understanding before packaging.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - An agency selling lead generation can list setup issues, ad fatigue, attribution confusion, follow-up bottlenecks, and what the buyer needs after leads arrive before deciding the deliverables.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Create Your Core Offer Part 1 & 2
- **Related Items:** None

### Add scarcity and urgency ethically
- **ID:** `100m-offers-checklist-006`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps buyers act sooner without weakening price through discounting.
- **How It Works:** The checklist pushes fixed caps, cohort timing, expiring bonuses, and time-sensitive opportunities to make the buyer decide. The emphasis is on bounded access rather than price-cutting.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A consultant could limit intake to five clients per month and attach a start-date deadline rather than discounting to force action.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Add Scarcity & Urgency
- **Related Items:** None

### Use bonuses to solve objections instead of discounting
- **ID:** `100m-offers-checklist-007`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps businesses increase yes-rates while preserving price and perceived value.
- **How It Works:** The checklist recommends stacking bonuses after price, giving them benefit-heavy names, assigning real value, and making them solve likely buyer objections. The bonus should make the core purchase easier to justify, not cheaper.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A software company could add migration support, KPI templates, and an implementation checklist as bonuses instead of cutting the subscription price.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Add Bonuses
- **Related Items:** None

## Strategic Ideas
### Use premium pricing as part of value creation
- **ID:** `100m-offers-checklist-002`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps operators understand that higher price can support both perception and results when the offer is real.
- **How It Works:** The checklist explicitly pushes the user to charge more than competitors and tie the price to value. The underlying logic is that higher price can increase buy-in, perceived seriousness, and the resources available to deliver results.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A coach can stop copying the cheapest market rate and instead raise price after strengthening proof, support, and promise clarity.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Charge What It’s Worth; Maximize Value Using Value Equation
- **Related Items:** None

## Frameworks
### Use the delivery cube to create higher-value versions
- **ID:** `100m-offers-checklist-004`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps businesses systematically vary effort, support, speed, medium, and format to increase value.
- **How It Works:** The delivery cube turns offer creation into a menu of delivery decisions rather than a single default service model. It helps generate options that can be sold at different value or cost levels.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A community product can test one-to-many recorded delivery for scale, then add higher-ticket done-with-you support via Zoom and chat for premium buyers.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: The Delivery Cube
- **Related Items:** None

### Trim low-value pieces and stack the strongest components
- **ID:** `100m-offers-checklist-005`
- **Problem(s) Solved:**
  - Helps businesses avoid bloated offers that are expensive to fulfill but not compelling to buy.
- **How It Works:** The checklist's trim-and-stack section recommends keeping only high-value elements, limiting high-cost pieces, and letting the offer feel substantial because of the right stack, not sheer volume.
- **Usage Example(s):**
  - A service firm can remove seven low-impact deliverables and instead package three strong value drivers plus one premium support element that actually closes sales.
- **Source Notes:** Sections: Trim & Stack
- **Related Items:** None
