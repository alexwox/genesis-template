## 3. Customer Discovery Case Studies

### 3A. Superhuman — Engineering Product-Market Fit

**Source:** Rahul Vohra, First Round Review, 2019 ([link](https://review.firstround.com/how-superhuman-built-an-engine-to-find-product-market-fit/))
**Year:** 2019 | **Confidence:** Observed

**Process:**
1. Adopted Sean Ellis's PMF metric: "How disappointed would you be if you could no longer use this product?"
2. Surveyed users with a 4-question survey (very disappointed / somewhat / not disappointed + segmentation + benefit + improvement questions)
3. Only ~40 respondents needed for directionally correct results
4. Segmented by "very disappointed" users to identify the ideal customer profile

**Results:**
- Started at **22% PMF score** (very disappointed)
- Iterated product based on survey feedback using a 50/50 roadmap: half doubling down on what power users loved, half removing blockers for "somewhat disappointed" users
- Improved to **58% PMF score** — well above the 40% threshold
- Framework has been adopted by hundreds of startups

**Key lesson:** Discovery doesn't have to be only interviews. A structured 4-question survey with just 40 responses, repeated quarterly, can drive systematic product improvement. The segmentation insight — focus on your happiest users, not all users — is critical.

### 3B. LeadSift — 3 Pivots to $1M ARR Through Structured Discovery

**Source:** SaaS Club podcast ([link](https://saasclub.io/podcast/saas-customer-discovery-tukan-das-leadsift/))
**Year:** 2019 | **Confidence:** Observed

**Process:**
1. Spent $1.8M and 3 years on two failed pivots (automotive brands, ad agencies)
2. With only 1 year of runway left, committed to structured discovery
3. Conducted **80+ prospect interviews** before writing code
4. Set a threshold: **80% validation** required before committing to development
5. Delivered value manually via spreadsheets while automating gradually
6. Secured **3 paying customers before any code was written**

**Results:**
- Within 17 months of final pivot: 105 customers, ~$1M ARR, 13% MoM growth
- Critical insight: the pain wasn't with brand marketers (original thesis) but with **B2B sales teams** — the entire positioning shifted

**Key lesson:** 80+ interviews before building, 80% validation threshold before committing, and securing payment before code are hallmarks of rigorous discovery. The positioning shift (marketers → sales teams) could only have been discovered through volume.

### 3C. Dropbox — Video MVP Validation

**Source:** Multiple: Enosta ([link](https://enosta.com/insights/dropbox-mvp)), Shortform ([link](https://www.shortform.com/blog/dropbox-mvp-explainer-video)), Andrew Chen ([link](https://andrewchen.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/08/dropbox-startuplessonslearned-100423230315-phpapp02.pdf))
**Year:** 2007-2008 | **Confidence:** Observed

**Process:**
1. Drew Houston created a 3-minute demo video instead of building a full product
2. Posted to Hacker News on April 5, 2007, with a landing page capturing email addresses
3. Video packed with Easter eggs targeted to the Digg/HN audience

**Results:**
- Waitlist grew from 5,000 to **75,000 overnight** after going viral on Digg
- Reached 1M users 7 months after public launch with **no advertising spend**
- Validated "value hypothesis" without requiring a functional product

**Key lesson:** Discovery can take many forms. Houston's video validated demand at scale, but it was a one-way broadcast, not a conversation. For stakeholder discovery specifically, this approach tests demand but NOT the granular workflow insights that interviews reveal.

### 3D. Intercom — Hand-Crafted Email Discovery

**Source:** Intercom Blog ([link](https://www.intercom.com/blog/blog/how-intercom-got-our-first-customers/))
**Year:** 2011 | **Confidence:** Observed

**Process:**
1. Des Traynor sent ~100 manually crafted emails per day
2. Deliberately avoided automation — each day's emails incorporated learnings from previous replies
3. ~40 responses per day, read and analyzed individually
4. 50% of founder time spent on customer communication
5. Conference appearances, blog comments, and HN discussions supplemented email

**Results:**
- This iterative, high-touch approach built Intercom's first 100 paying customers
- Each daily batch was an iteration on messaging and positioning
- The learning loop (100 emails → 40 responses → updated messaging next day) compressed discovery into a continuous feedback system

**Key lesson:** Manual outreach at modest scale (100/day) with tight feedback loops outperforms automated blast-and-pray. The daily iteration cycle mirrors the stakeholder discovery skill's recommendation to update hypotheses after each interview batch.

## 5. Implications for the Stakeholder Discovery Skill

### What the data validates in the current skill:
1. **20-30 target list is correct.** Academic saturation research says 9-17 interviews for homogeneous groups. With a ~30% interview booking rate, targeting 30 people should yield 9-12 completed interviews — right in the saturation zone.
2. **4-channel approach is correct.** Conference speakers are the highest-response channel (25-40%). LinkedIn connect+message is second (6-14% net). Cold email is weakest and declining.
3. **"Research framing, never customer discovery"** is validated. Research-framed outreach triggers reciprocity, not sales resistance. The value nugget (benchmark report) aligns with practitioner motivation.
4. **"Ask for stories, not opinions"** is the #1 practitioner recommendation across Mom Test, YC, First Round Review, and Torres.
5. **10 interviews → pattern emergence** aligns with Guest et al. (2006) finding saturation at 12 and metathemes at 6.
6. **Consultant Backdoor (3-5 consultants)** is supported: consultants score highest on insight density, and 6 interviews capture high-level metathemes.

### What could be added or sharpened:
1. **Channel-specific response rate expectations** so users can plan their target list size correctly. If booking rate is 15%, you need 80 targets; if 30%, you need 40.
2. **The 3-interview stop rule** as a concrete heuristic for when to stop interviewing.
3. **Explicit "bad data" warnings** — compliments, fluff, hypotheticals — during interview prep.
4. **The "hire somebody" reframe** as an alternative opening to "Walk me through how you currently handle [process]."
5. **48-hour follow-up rule** for conference contacts.
6. **The Superhuman PMF survey** as a post-interview quantitative validation step (40 responses, 40% threshold).
7. **An 80% validation threshold** before committing to building (LeadSift precedent).
